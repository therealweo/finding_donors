Transforming Skewed Continuous Features¶

A dataset may sometimes contain at least one feature whose values tend to lie near a single number, but will also have a non-trivial number of vastly larger or smaller values than that single number. Algorithms can be sensitive to such distributions of values and can underperform if the range is not properly normalized. With the census dataset two features fit this description: 'capital-gain' and 'capital-loss'.

Run the code cell below to plot a histogram of these two features. Note the range of the values present and how they are distributed.

For highly-skewed feature distributions such as 'capital-gain' and 'capital-loss', it is common practice to apply a logarithmic transformation on the data so that the very large and very small values do not negatively affect the performance of a learning algorithm. Using a logarithmic transformation significantly reduces the range of values caused by outliers. Care must be taken when applying this transformation however: The logarithm of 0 is undefined, so we must translate the values by a small amount above 0 to apply the the logarithm successfully.

Metrics and the Naive Predictor¶

CharityML, equipped with their research, knows individuals that make more than \$50,000 are most likely to donate to their charity. Because of this, *UdacityML* is particularly interested in predicting who makes more than \$50,000 accurately. It would seem that using accuracy as a metric for evaluating a particular model's performace would is appropriate. Additionally, identifying someone that does not make more than \$50,000 as someone who does would be detrimental to *UdacityML*, since they are looking to find individuals willing to donate. Therefore, a model's ability to precisely predict those that make more than \$50,000 is more important than the model's ability to recall those individuals. We can use F-beta score as a metric that considers both precision and recall:

$$ F_{\beta} = (1 + \beta^2) \cdot \frac{precision \cdot recall}{\left( \beta^2 \cdot precision \right) + recall} $$

In particular, when $\beta = 0.5$, more emphasis is placed on precision. This is called the F$_{0.5}$ score (or F-score for simplicity).

Looking at the distribution of classes (those who make at most \$50,000, and those who make more), it's clear most individuals do not make more than \$50,000. This can greatly affect accuracy, since we could simply say "this person does not make more than \$50,000" and generally be right, without ever looking at the data! Making such a statement would be called naive, since we have not considered any information to substantiate the claim. It is always important to consider the naive prediction for your data, to help establish a benchmark for whether a model is performing well. That been said, using that prediction would be pointless: If we predicted all people made less than \$50,000, UdacityML would identify no one as donors.

beta = 0.5
n_true_pos = len(pred[income==pred][pred==1])
n_false_pos = len(pred[income!=pred][pred==1])
n_true_neg = len(pred[income==pred][pred==0])
n_false_neg = len(pred[income!=pred][pred==0])
precision = n_true_pos / float(n_true_pos + n_false_pos)
recall = n_true_pos / float(n_true_pos + n_false_neg)
fscore = (1 + beta**2)*(precision*recall/((beta**2*precision) + recall))

Decision trees generalize rules from the training data in order to create a lightweight, efficient algorithm for classifying new examples. Although prone to overfitting if allowed to grow complex, decision trees are easy to understand and interpret, selectively use the most relevant features of the data, and are applicable to a wide range of fields from astronomy to pharmacology (a selection of applications can be found here.) Examination of our data suggests that some features may be more relevant to the label than others--for example, education level seems more likely to have an impact on income than marital status--and so a decision tree may be a good choice for picking out the most important factors in CharityML's potential donors.

AdaBoost is an ensemble boosting algorithm. It creates many classifiers in sequence, each of which gives more weight to training examples which the previous instance misclassified. This approach has several significant advantages. Because the algorithm targets its own weaknesses, it can handle tasks on which traditional approaches do not perform well. It is unusually resistant to overfitting as well, so long as the underlying classifier is not prone to overfitting. On the other hand, ensemble methods are more computationally expensive, because they require the training and use of many classifiers. Ensemble methods have been used in the real world to tackle a variety of difficult problems, including remote detection of landscape features from satellite imagery, network intrusion detection, identification of a particular person, and medical applications to classify MRIs, ECGs, and other. This paper by Nikunj Oza and Kagan Tumer contains more information about how ensemble methods apply to each of these domains. In the case of CharityML, we can expect AdaBoost to perform well because it is a good generalist, as long as we are willing to put up with the possibility of slower training and predicting times.

Support Vector Machines, or SVMs, maximize the margin between its classes using only the points closest to the decision boundary. They are especially effective in spaces with many dimensions, of which our 103-feature dataset is one. While SVMs become less effective in the case that the number of features is much greater than the number of samples, that isn't a problem here. Finally, SVMs are versatile in the case that our data aren't linearly separable, since they can use various kernel functions to separate data that are linearly married. Although they are no longer the state of the art for most applications, SVMs were applied to a wide range of applications in the 1990s and early 2000s. Problems they have been applied to include face detection in images, text classification, and more.

In one to two paragraphs, explain to CharityML, in layman's terms, how the final model chosen is supposed to work. Answer: AdaBoost begins by building a simple decision tree, a model that asks one or two questions about the "features" (e.g. age, occupation, years of education, etc) of a particular individual in order to determine whether they are likely to have an income of greater than \$50,000. It determines which questions to ask by looking at the features of individuals across our dataset and seeing how the profile of someone with an income over \$50,000 differs from that of someone who makes less.

Once the first decision tree is trained, AdaBoost uses it to evaluate all the individuals in our dataset, noting places where it comes up with the wrong answer. Next, AdaBoost builds another decision tree by the same process, but this time with special emphasis on correcting its previous errors. So, if the first decision tree predicted that someone would make less than \$50,000 but in fact they made more, the new decision tree will try to find questions that will correctly identify that person as making more than \$50,000, even if that will make it misclassify another individual which it previously got right. AdaBoost repeats this process, building new decision trees that correct their predecessors' mistakes, around 50 times.

When the entire ensemble of trees is trained, it can be used to make good predictions by allowing each simple tree to vote on a particular individual's status. Even though the individual trees are quite simple, asking only one or two questions about each individual, the ensemble is able to provide fairly sophisticated judgment and an improved accuracy rate over any of its parts.

Feature Selection¶

How does a model perform if we only use a subset of all the available features in the data? With less features required to train, the expectation is that training and prediction time is much lower at the cost of performance metrics. From the visualization above, we see that the top five most important features contribute more than half of the importance of all features present in the data. This hints that we can attempt to reduce the feature space and simplify the information required for the model to learn.
